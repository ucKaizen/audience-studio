What if we could simulate?

A simulation layer on top of the reach and ratings data broadcasters and brands already trust

Today, broadcasters and brands work with rich reach and ratings data — panels, surveys, and fusion. That data underpins the confident decisions they make every day.

Where a simulation layer on top of that dataset would unlock new value:

  • Detailed reads on narrow audience segments
  • Future programmes, creative, and timing — rehearsed before they air or launch
  • “What would happen if…” scenarios on alternative strategies, audiences, or dates
  • Populations and events that aren’t practical to measure directly
What if we could fill those gaps — or simulate an entire population — almost realistically, at scale, in hours?

A spectrum of use — from gap-fill to full simulation

Run alongside a real study, plug a missing piece, or replace it entirely.

Mode A

Gap-fill

Run alongside an existing study to plug a missing piece.

When to use

  • Cells too thin to report
  • Scenario not tested by the study
  • Variant not included in the original brief
  • Forward-looking forecast where data does not yet exist
Mode B

Full simulation

Run as a standalone simulation when speed or breadth matters.

When to use

  • A rapid read on a new idea or proposition
  • Exploratory questions where speed and breadth are most useful
  • Niche, sensitive, or future-state subjects
  • “What would happen if…” rehearsals on alternative strategies, audiences, or dates

Ingredient 1 — a credible graph

  • Built on consented panel, survey, ownership, and interest data
  • Typed relationships — not free-form, not LLM-extracted
  • Schema-validated — same data in, same graph out

Ingredient 2 — rules we control

  • Clear rules for who reacts to what, when, and why — declared up front
  • Numbers we can trace back to the inputs — not made up by an LLM
  • Auditable trace — every decision logged with reason

Agents

Census pool · synthetic fit · library curation

  • Full population pool — every agent the data supports, kept as a control.
  • The right subset for the question — pick agents relevant to the brief; criteria are declared up front so the answer can’t be hand-picked. An opposite-shaped subset is run alongside as a sanity check.
  • Reusable groups — once an agent group has answered known questions accurately, we keep that group as the go-to for similar questions. Refreshed regularly so they don’t go stale.

What this simulation does not account for

Honest disclosure on what’s out of scope for this illustrative demo:

  • Price elasticity — would normally come from a price-test or conjoint study
  • Promotion & shelf effects — depth of discount, end-cap placement, share-of-shelf
  • Competitive context — what competing products do at the same moment
  • Distribution & availability — assumes homogeneous availability across the panel
  • Media exposure modelling — assumes the ad is seen; doesn’t simulate media plan or reach build

These are the engine’s declared ceilings, captured on the Risks & Unknowns slide. The funnel above models persona reception of the creative — the engine’s strongest capability. Closing each of these gaps is on the roadmap.

Risks and unknowns

The questions we expect to be asked in this room — with our honest answers

  1. Consent and second-use. Panel consent may not cover building agents from the data. Legal review needed before any commercial use.
  2. Cherry-picking risk. Choosing the right subset of agents could pre-decide the answer. We declare the criteria up front and run an opposite-shaped group alongside as a sanity check.
  3. Doesn’t transfer for free. Trust earned on one type of question doesn’t carry over to a different type — each needs its own validation.
  4. Things change. What worked last year may not work this year. Agent groups need refreshing on a regular schedule.
  5. Group answers only. We claim accuracy for the population averages, not for any single agent.
  6. What it can’t do yet. Price, shelf placement, distribution — these aren’t modelled. Today it’s strong on creative reception, not on commercial conditions.
  7. Model drift. When the underlying LLM is upgraded the numbers can shift. We test across model versions and freeze inputs to minimise this.
  8. Same data builds and judges it. The panel is used to build the agents and to validate them. We keep some panel data aside so it never trains on what it’s tested against.
  9. Crowded market. Lots of “AI consumer” demos out there. What sets us apart is the data underneath, not the engine.
  10. Pricing model. Per-study, per-question, per-decision — we don’t know yet what buyers will accept.

What we need next

Two things have to validate together — the technology, and the commercial fit

Technology validation

does it work?

  • Run on a known case — compare against a real Brand Lift or Sales Lift result
  • Repeat for a second case in a different category
  • Publish the gap between simulated and measured numbers

Commercial validation

does anyone want it?

  • A sponsoring business unit prepared to put one real question through it
  • A client willing to share a real brief in exchange for the read
  • A pricing point that a buyer agrees to before we build further
The commercial side is the harder of the two. Building it is well understood; selling it depends on finding the buyer who values the speed enough to back it.
